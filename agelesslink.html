ageless link;

Ageless Link, in partnership with Ageless Alliance, is a nonprofit website solution that bridges volunteers with isolated elders in Orange County as a prevention for elder abuse, neglect, and exploitation. This project came from my senior design course at UC Irvine.

Our solution pairs volunteers with elders, allows volunteers to record their service hours, and gives the administrator access to a dashboard that provides an overview of volunteer activity which includes logged service hours, new volunteer applications, and volunteer-elder matches.

Role: As a UX Designer, I conducted user research, produced sketches and wireframes using Axure, and helped with web development (Bootstrap, HTML, and CSS.)

what's the problem?

The founder of Ageless Alliance initially asked for a creative software solution (computer and phone use) that collects information from elders and from volunteers. I felt that this was a very vague request — was there a current method of collecting information that didn't work? What about it wasn't working? A peer and I decided to meet with the founder and ask her questions to learn more about her organization and to identify the problem(s).

Our research on Ageless Alliance began with interviewing the founder and the social media manager/admin. They explained the mission of the organization and their current involvement with the elderly community. We learned that the organization's most valuable resource was its volunteer group which devotes time to assist elders with everyday tasks and most importantly, to keep elders company during certain days of the week. We asked about the challenges they were facing in managing the organization and discovered that these were in managing volunteer groups and member information. We learned that:

  1. It was hard to keep track of all elder-volunteer matches as they were manually done
  2. There was no visibility into volunteer activity and the volunteers did not always log in hours so the organization assumed they didn't visit the elder
  3. The volunteers only knew who and when to visit after receiving a call from a lead member
  4. The volunteers weren't always able to drive out to visit the elder which made some elders feel sad

We learned that the founder wanted an improved way to match elders with volunteers (these matches are the 'information' she was referring to in her initial request), but that she really needed a better way to direct and track volunteer activity. The problem then became clear: How can we create and track volunteer-elder matches?

After discussion, my team and our partner agreed that a website (portal) would benefit the organization greatly. Volunteers would use it to log in their hours and to see who they had been matched with. They would also be able to log their hours on the go via their mobile device. The administrator(s) would access a dashboard of volunteer activity. Since it was to be a website, it was important that we also include information about the organization and how to become a volunteer.

Prior to beginning the design of our website, I did a competitive analysis of similar sites to become aware of what other sites had content-wise and to see what worked and what didn't. I looked at three organizations: Elder Helpers, Caregivers, and Life Elder Care. Below is one of them:

meeting our users

Now that we had a clear view of our problem, the next step was to gain a clear understanding of our users: the volunteers and the administrator(s). We did not include elders for two reasons: (a) although many elders are technologically savvy, not all are and we did not want to risk the possibility of excluding some of our elders from being a part of this program, and (b) all relevant elder information is already held by the organization, so there's no need for an elder to have an account.

I helped conduct interviews with 5 volunteers to learn more about what it's like to be a volunteer and to identify pain points in the volunteer routine. Our volunteers were men and women of ages 40-54 who felt comfortable using the internet, computers, and mobile devices.

volunteers

key insights and opportunities

VOLUNTEERS

How might we... make it easy for volunteers to log in hours?

  • Volunteers have other responsibilities and driving to the office to log hours can be inconvenient

How might we... let volunteers know who to contact for assistance?

  • Depending on the situation, volunteers may need to call or message. Establishing a 'help' center can direct volunteers to the right people

How might we... inform volunteers of their duties as a volunteer?

  • Volunteers are given a short guide to what's expected of them, but establishing a comprehensive online guide may help both to inform the volunteer and to reduce paper use

administrator

key insights and opportunities

ADMINISTRATOR

How might we... assist the admin in matching and tracking volunteer-elder pairs?

  • If we can automatically pair volunteers with elders, we can help the administrator devote her time to other important tasks

How might we... simplify the process of processing volunteer applications?

  • Applying online would rid of paper applications and make it easier to keep track of which ones need to be processed

How might we... give the admin visibility into volunteer activity?

  • If we can notify the admin about new applicants, hibernating volunteers, active volunteers, etc. then we can make it less stressful for leadership to run the organization

reflection

The biggest challenge was figuring out how to start because our partner wasn't too sure of what she was looking for. This project taught me that research is imperative to projects like this because not only do we learn about the organization, the client, and the users, but we also unearth needs that the client may not have been aware of.

Another challenge was that of web development. Before this, I had done a little bit in HTML and CSS, but I never actually put something significant together. It was a big learning opportunity for me.

My takeaway from this experience is: we can't proceed with assumptions in design. If a UI mockup isn't in line with what users require, then it's just a UI mockup. It's not a solution. I learned to always ask questions if I'm feeling unsure. I also learned that checking in with the client (and/or users) to validate progress helps avoid major issues that would require valuable time and resources to fix later on.
