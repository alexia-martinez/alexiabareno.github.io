inventory app;

I conducted a pilot study that looked into establishing a uniform monthly inventory count process for company branches nationwide. I studied my supervisor's proposed solution of designing an app that would help managers scan inventory items into an exportable spreadsheet. I divided my study into the following phases: user research, information architecture, design, development, and testing. This project came from my internship.

In compliance with my non-disclosure agreement, I have omitted and obfuscated confidential information from this case study.

Role: As a UX Designer, I conducted user research and interviews, created flows and UI designs using Axure, and conducted usability tests.

research

Problem: branches have different ways of doing inventory which makes it difficult to address issues in reported inventory numbers.

To begin this project, I recruited seven managers to be in my pilot group: two were in California and the rest were in Illinois, Georgia, Texas, Lousiana, and Virginia. Before diving into design, I spent a day interviewing the group to learn about their current practices when it came to doing inventory. The interview was conducted over the phone.

The types of questions asked revolved around the themes of organization, process, and tools. Some of these questions were:

Here is some of what I learned:

  • Larger branches had come up with 'shortcuts' to speed up inventory counts
  • One of the managers had created a macro-enabled spreadsheet that identifies missing inventory
  • 86% of the group was doing inventory using pencil and paper
  • Two of the managers weren't too comfortable with technology
  • All branches had inventory organized based on item type
  • Smaller branches took about 20-30 mins to do inventory; larger branches took 45min-1hr+
  • Branches once used inventory tracking software - now very outdated, did not upgrade because not cost effective

After the call, I spent another day organizing my data to present to my supervisor.

Hypothesis: the inventory app will accomodate all branches regardless of size by cutting down the time it takes branches to count their stock.

My supervisor had agreed that we had a good case to pursue her idea and develop the app. As an addition to the new process, she decided to include the manager's macro-enabled Excel file (TVM audit) as another tool to be used. The audit worked using two files: an Excel download from the company's system which included all inventory owned by a branch and an Excel file produced by the app containing the scanned items present in the warehouse. The audit then checked both files and highlighted items that weren't present in either list which meant they could be missing.

Since I am not a visual designer, I used elements of Material Design and followed it as a guideline. All branch managers and warehouse workers were given an Android company phone so I felt it was appropriate to design the app using Material Design.

I made a change during this phase by including a 'continuous scan' feature that allowed managers to scan items with the same tag/item number so that they wouldn't have to type it in for each scan.

development

I worked with another IT Intern who was comfortable with app development. Since he was working in LA and I in Huntington Beach, we would meet once a week so that I could check in with him. Together, we'd go over his work to make sure that it was reflective of the requirements I had given him.

It took about three weeks for him to finish the first prototype of the app. After getting approval from our supervisors, I proceeded to test it with my pilot group.

testing

I ordered several bluetooth barcode scanners to be paired up with the Samsung phones and to be used with the app.

The tasks for testing were very simple:

  1. Download the inventory list from the company system
  2. Scan 20 items from the warehouse
  3. Export the spreadsheet from the app
  4. Upload the files from steps 1 and 2 to the audit file and run the macro

Since I wasn't able to oversee testing, I had to conduct these tests remotely by handing over the instructions and tools and waiting a week to hear back from the managers. It wasn't the best way to do testing, but it was the only way.

I conducted another group call to learn about their thoughts and suggestions for improvement. One of the requested changes was that the 'Back' button be moved towards the bottom of the screen because it felt odd to have it towards the top. I agreed that it was awkwardly placed and later realized that by having it towards the bottom, the manager could use their thumb (while holding their phone in one hand) to go back.

reflection

I was happy to learn that the app was received positively among the group. Unfortunately, because of the priority of other projects, I was unable to pursue the launch of this new process among other branches. It did feel amazing, however, to know that the branches I had worked with during the study decided to continue using the new process.

I would have changed two things about this project. First, I would have liked to have worked with the other IT intern from the start because I think he would have felt more comfortable working with me and he might have felt more ownership of the project rather than it just being a handoff.

Second, I would have liked to conduct the testing in person to observe the managers as they used the app and determine if it fit well within the inventory process. I feel like opportunities for insight were lost in having done testing the way we did, but I understand that sometimes situations are not ideal and that's ok. It's about making the best out of what has been given.

I'm very grateful for this experience because it made me confirm my desire to enter the UX field. It's very fulfilling to know that my efforts made someone's job easier and more enjoyable.
